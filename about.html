Who was Thucydides?

Thucydides (c. 460 - 400 BC) was an Athenian historian and general.

Thucydides is often referred to as the "father of scientific history" for the metholodogy found in his most famous work, the History of the Peloponnesian War, an account of the massive war between Athens and Sparta.

Not much is known about his life, save for what he writes in his own work.

According to him, he was born in Athens to a family with blood and business ties to Thrace.

Due to this connection, Thucydides was sent as a general to the nearby island of Thasos in 424 BC.

He would defend the major city of Amphipolis from the Spartan general Brasidas, but despite his best efforts, the city fell.

For his failure, Thucydides was exiled from Athens, and he decided to use the chance to travel around the Spartan-allied states, allowing him to see the conflict from both sides and record it.

He pioneered many critical elements of historiography today, including:

  • Strict chronological organization
  • Extensive use of primary sources
  • Neutrality in accounts, despite being Athenian himself
  • Disregarding the role of the supernatural in events
  • Rationalization and historicization of mythology

This is in stark contrast to his contemporaries, such as Herodotus, who often included the supernatural or popular mythology in their work.
